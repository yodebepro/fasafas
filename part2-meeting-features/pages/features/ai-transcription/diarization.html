Speaker Diarization

Speaker diarization labels each transcript line with who was speaking — automatically, without any setup. Smart Recognition then maps speaker IDs to real names using voice registration.

How Speaker Diarization Works
  1. Individual audio tracks captured per participant
    Instead of mixing all audio, the SFU provides separate audio tracks per participant to the diarization pipeline. This is the most accurate approach — no need to separate voices post-mix.
  2. Pyannote.audio 3.0 assigns speaker segments
    Pyannote analyzes each track and produces speaker segments: "Speaker A: 0:00–0:12, Speaker B: 0:14–0:28..." with millisecond precision.
  3. Speaker IDs assigned: Speaker 1, Speaker 2, etc.
    Without Smart Recognition, speakers are labeled generically. The ID is consistent throughout the meeting — "Speaker 1" in minute 3 is the same person as "Speaker 1" in minute 40.
  4. Segments merged with Whisper transcript
    Each Whisper word timestamp is matched to a speaker segment. The combined result: "Speaker 1 [00:02:14]: The budget for Q3 is approved."
  5. Retroactive correction if speaker ID changes
    If Pyannote initially assigns two segments to different speakers but later determines they're the same (based on longer voice sample), it retroactively corrects the earlier segments.
Diarization Quality Factors
What Helps Accuracy
  • Each participant uses their own dedicated microphone (not speakerphone or shared mic)
  • Clear turn-taking — one person speaks at a time
  • Good audio quality (minimal background noise, no echo)
  • Smart Recognition enrolled (maps IDs to real names)
  • Meetings under 2 hours (cumulative model drift in very long sessions)
What Hurts Accuracy
  • Shared microphones (conference room speakerphone) — multiple people per mic channel
  • Overlapping speech — two people talking simultaneously confuses segment assignment
  • Background talkers (TVs, radio) can be classified as a unique speaker
  • Very similar voice types (e.g., identical twins on the same call)
  • Participants speaking in different rooms with vastly different acoustic environments
