SFU vs P2P vs MCU

Three architectures exist for multi-party video calls. Each has different tradeoffs in scalability, latency, cost, and quality. FasaFas uses SFU for the best balance of all four.

Why FasaFas Uses SFU
  • Scales to 500+ participants — each participant uploads once; SFU routes to everyone
  • Low latency — no decode/re-encode cycle; media passes through in ~20–50ms
  • Individual stream per subscriber — each person gets the quality their network supports (simulcast)
  • AI features work server-side — SFU can tap the audio stream for transcription without touching encryption
  • Server-side recording — SFU writes raw tracks to storage without client involvement
  • Cost-efficient — no GPU for video decode/encode on the server; routing is CPU-cheap
SFU Limitations
  • Download grows with N — in a 500-person meeting, you might download 49 streams (visible tiles); P2P would be worse, but MCU would be just 1 stream. FasaFas mitigates with lazy-load and simulcast.
  • Not truly E2E encrypted by default — SFU must see the packets to route them. E2E mode exists but disables AI and recording.
  • Single point of failure risk — mitigated by redundant SFU clusters, active health checks, and automatic failover.
  • TURN relay adds latency — when direct path isn't possible (20–30% of enterprise users), TURN relay adds 20–40ms.
