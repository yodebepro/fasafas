Adaptive Bitrate

Adaptive bitrate streaming ensures everyone sees the best video quality their network can support — automatically, invisibly, and without ever freezing or dropping the call.

Adaptive Bitrate — How It Works
  1. You send 3 streams simultaneously (Simulcast)
    Your browser encodes your video at 1080p, 480p, and 240p at the same time and sends all three to the SFU. Total upload: ~3.5 Mbps.
  2. SFU monitors each subscriber's network
    Using REMB (Receiver Estimated Max Bitrate) and TWCC (Transport Wide Congestion Control) feedback packets, the SFU knows exactly how much bandwidth each subscriber can receive in real time.
  3. SFU selects the right layer per subscriber
    If subscriber A has 3 Mbps download → gets your 1080p layer. If subscriber B has 400 kbps → gets your 240p layer. Each subscriber gets an independent stream.
  4. Switch happens seamlessly
    If your network degrades mid-call, the SFU switches to a lower layer for your stream. No freeze, no rebuffering — just a brief quality change. Typically imperceptible.

VP9 SVC — Even More Efficient
On Chrome and Edge, FasaFas uses VP9 Scalable Video Coding (SVC) instead of simulcast. SVC embeds all three quality layers in a single stream — saving upload bandwidth while giving the SFU the same flexibility to forward any layer.
How the SFU Measures Your Network
REMB (Receiver Estimated Max Bitrate)
  • Each subscriber sends REMB feedback to the SFU every 1 second
  • Reports the maximum bitrate it believes it can receive
  • Calculated by measuring actual received packet rate vs expected rate
  • SFU reads REMB → picks simulcast layer accordingly
  • If REMB drops below 800 kbps → switch from 1080p to 480p
  • If REMB drops below 200 kbps → switch from 480p to 240p
TWCC (Transport Wide Congestion Control)
  • More modern and accurate than REMB
  • Sender uses feedback to estimate path capacity (not just receive side)
  • Implemented in Chrome, Edge, Firefox (not Safari)
  • Allows probing: sender temporarily increases bitrate to test headroom
  • Reacts to congestion 2–3× faster than REMB-only systems
  • Enables smoother quality transitions with less visible switching
